Find something you actually care about — from Kaggle, data.gov, your state's open data portal, a sports stats site, a Wikipedia table you can paste into Sheets. To count, it must meet all of these:

  • At least 200 rows (so groups have real counts)
  • At least 3 categorical columns you could group by (text values like "Country", "Genre", "Decade")

  • You can actually download it as CSV or Excel — not just view it on a webpage
  • The source is cited and credible (a name, a URL, a date)
Not allowed: the Michigan crash data from Days 1–3 (we already analyzed it), survey data you generated yourself with under 50 responses, or scraped data without a clear source.

Step 2 — frame your questions

What makes a good pivot-table question?
A good question has the shape: "For [filter / subset], how does [numeric thing] vary by [categorical thing]?"

That shape maps directly to a pivot table: the filter goes in Filters, the categorical thing goes in Rows (or Columns), and the numeric thing goes in Values.

Examples of good questions:
  • "Among Olympic gold medalists, how does average age vary by sport?"
  • "For songs from 1960–2020, how has average tempo changed by decade?"
  • "Which colleges have the best graduation rate per dollar of tuition?"
Bad questions (and why):
  • "What's interesting in this data?" — too vague, no pivot maps to it
  • "Are basketball players tall?" — answer is obvious, no surprise possible
  • "Why do people get heart disease?" — pivots show what, not why
  • "How many rows are there?" — that's a count, not an analysis
Brainstorm — write three candidate questions

Write three different questions you could ask about your dataset. Don't filter yet — get them on paper, then pick. Use the "For ___, how does ___ vary by ___?" shape.

Pick one and sharpen it

Which of your three feels most interesting AND is actually answerable with your columns? Restate it as a single clear question.

Step 3 — analyze with pivot tables

Get your data into Sheets
  1. Download the dataset as CSV or Excel (.xlsx) from your source.
  2. If the file is huge (over 50,000 rows), open it in Excel or Sheets and trim it. Take a clean subset — e.g., one country, one decade, one league — that still has 200+ rows.
  3. In Google Sheets: File → Import → Upload, then Replace spreadsheet, separator Comma.
  4. Check your import: Row 1 should be column headers. Numeric columns should be right-aligned (numbers, not text). Date columns should sort as dates.
If a numeric column shows up as text: select the column, then Format → Number → Number. Pivots can only sum/average actual numbers.
